
## The problem with being a solution machine

Most designers, when given a brief, start building. They treat the question as given and optimize the answer.

That's the wrong instinct.

The brief is almost never the real problem. A client asking for a better search experience might actually have an information architecture problem. A request for a new onboarding flow might really be a product definition problem. A "redesign our website" brief often hides a fundamental question about what the organization is actually offering and to whom.

If you start with screens, you've already accepted the wrong framing. You've become a solution machine — and solution machines get replaced.

---

## Why I start with an object model, not screens

Every project I start the same way: before any wireframe, before any flow, I map the objects.

What are the core things in this system? What do users call them — and what does the business call them? How do they relate? What can a user do with each one? What does the business need to happen?

This isn't UML. It's not a deliverable that ships. It's a thinking tool that forces the hard questions early, when changing your mind is cheap.

When you understand the objects and their relationships, the screens become obvious. When you skip that step, you're making structural decisions implicitly — usually wrong, always invisible, always expensive to undo.

---

## What AI actually reveals

AI can generate interfaces. It can produce wireframes, suggest copy, iterate on layouts faster than any human. The execution layer is increasingly automated.

What AI can't do is decide what a product should fundamentally be. It can't tell you which objects matter, which relationships are real, which user needs are worth solving or which business constraints are non-negotiable. It can't sit in a room with a client and understand what they haven't said yet.

That judgment — structural, architectural, grounded in both the user and the business — is what makes a UX designer irreplaceable. It was always the job. AI just stripped away everything that was hiding it.

The generalist isn't coming back. The architect never left.

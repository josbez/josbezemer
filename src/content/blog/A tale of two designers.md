The discourse these days is that design is changing, Claude Design being the most recent example, AI has absolutely impacted the way we're working far more and faster than we envisioned. But the tools that you use to create the solutions to the problems you're facing are not what design is. 

To me design has always been about solving problems people have. I argue that design isn't changing at all. Designers used to have only one path to walk on, but now there's two. Here's the tale of two designers.

---
## The two designers
I remember when this article went viral, [The Dribblification of Design](https://www.intercom.com/blog/the-dribbblisation-of-design/). Whilst it seems Dribble is quite dead and the "art" of pixel pushing is long behind us, this is what drove a lot of people to design and what a lot of designers align themselves with. Tho probably more positively framed. The problem here for me lies in the fact that these designs don't service a problem being solved. 

When someone uses Claude Design, Google Stitch, Figma Make or even Lovable to prompt a solution, then that's what you'll get. That exact solution. It looks good enough, probably works on a surface level. But are you even solving the correct solution? And that's where your value as designer lies; defining the problems and then vocalising the solution. 

There are two ways how to vocalise these solutions: either the solutions already exist, or they don't exist yet. Designers should be able to **judge** these (more on that in a different post).

When the designer has acknowledged that the solution already exists, a tool like Stich or Claude Design relies on proper prompting the problem and solution framing to get the correct results from these tools. When the solution doesn't exist yet a designer uses different tool, Figma, Axure, pen and paper, sharpies on a whiteboard and "prompts" (visualises) the solution. These are the two routes a designer takes and chooses their tools accordingly. Without generational tools there was only one route, which took a hell of a long time grinding out the same square boxes over and over again to visualise the same solutions. This wasted time has been reduced.

Here lies the classic of design thinking: the double diamond. It's been called dead, been revived and killed again by more people you can count and more times than I care to prompt. But it's exactly what a designer goes trough each and every design process.
